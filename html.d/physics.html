Bachelor’s thesis

Polarized DIS and Quark-Gluon-Quark-Contributions to the Wandzura-Wilczek-Relation

Summary.

Since the first deep inelastic scattering (DIS) experiments in the late 1960’s, the differential scattering cross-sections (DSCSs) associated with this process are measured. These quantities are expressed in terms of so-called structure functions, which encode information on the scattering process. From the DSCS measurements these structure functions can be extracted. All of this is done without knowledge about partons or, generally, the target’s (e.g., proton’s) internal structure. On the other hand, predictions for the DSCSs can also be made based on quantum chromo dynamics (QCD). This leads to an expression for the DSCS in terms of so-called parton distributions, which are directly linked to QCD matrix elements. Comparing the two different descriptions yields important relations between the structure functions, which are obtained through the parton picture. The most prominent example for such a relation is the Callan-Gross relation, connecting the unpolarised DIS structure functions. Generally speaking, relations between structure functions can be tested experimentally, thereby enabling tests of fundamental theories such as QCD, which describe some of the most fundamental processes in our universe.

In this thesis we consider polarised DIS and focus on the well-known Wandzura-Wilczek relation. We derive twist-3 contibutions which break this relation and express them in yet different distributions, which are linked to quark-gluon-quark correlators. For these distributions we test a physically motivated model by fitting the free parameters to experimental data, via the programming language FORmula TRANslator (FOTRAN). With the obtained model patameters, we are able to visualise these distributions, thereby unveiling their properties.

Master’s thesis

Quantum Simulation of Molecular Dynamics in Rydberg Atom Arrays

Abstract.

Rydberg atoms held in optical tweezers feature electronic and vibrational degrees of freedom which can be brought into interaction in a controllable way. Therefore, these systems enable the investigation of phenomena, similar to those studied in molecular physics, but on exaggerated length and time scales. We exploit these interactions to investigate the possibility of building a quantum simulation framework for molecular systems. In our framework, we find that beyond certain coupling strengths, the vibrational motion becomes unstable, and we derive the critical values. Moreover, we derive quantum corrections to the ground state energy of a micrometer-sized diatomic and triangular atom array, which are not captured by the Born-Oppenheimer approximation. We investigate the physical implications of these corrections and find that they are linked to phonon-squeezed states. We further establish their impact on a regime where the triangular array is in a superposition of distorted triangular shapes (Jahn-Teller regime). Finally, we propose a protocol to prepare molecular states whose structure is strongly affected by electron-phonon coupling. Our results show that trapped Rydberg atom arrays indeed offer a versatile platform for the study of quantum phenomena that link to molecular physics.

Publication

Rydberg atom arrays as quantum simulators for molecular dynamics

Abstract.

Rydberg atoms held in optical tweezer arrays combine vibrational and electronic degrees of freedom that can be coupled and manipulated at a microscopic level. This opens opportunities for the quantum simulation of artificial molecular systems and offers in particular a platform for probing complex vibronic dynamics in controlled settings with increasing complexity. Tailored interatomic interactions and electron-phonon couplings yield handles for designing electronic state manifolds, for studying structural transitions, and for exploring non-classical vibrational states near molecular instabilities. Furthermore, this quantum simulator opens opportunities for testing and quantifying the validity of fundamental concepts, such as the Born-Oppenheimer approximation and quantum corrections to it.

Reference: Phys. Rev. Research 7, L042009
Preprint: arXiv [quant-ph], 2506.90865v1 (2025)
